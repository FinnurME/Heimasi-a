Assignment 2

Computer aided cutting

Assignment 2 is divided in two parts

  • Part 1: Make something using the vinlycutter with a max area of 100x50cm.
  • Part 2: Design a parametric pressfit model of building units. The model needs to be scaleable in different dimensions using parameters with softwere of your own choice.

Part 1

As a frisbee golf player, It would be practical for me to use this opurtunity to cut out vinyl templates for colouring my discs. I want have two designs. The other design is meant for a disc called Thunderbird and the design for it is a character from the video game Enter the Gungeon. The other design is for a disc called Relay and is a few orbs meant to be a sun and a few planets that are aesthetically pleasing. The two pictures I want to use as templates are the following:

Template 1 (planets) Template 2 (Beholder)

These pictures were uploaded into Inkscape and the outlines selected. The thickness of them was set to 0.02 mm and the file exported as a pdf-file. The material used was a matte black vinyl.

Vinyl cutouts

Masking tape was used to transport the cutout from the white sheet to the disc. Before sticking the vinly to the disc, it was cleand with soap and water, dried and then cleaned again with isopropyl alcohol. The vinyl was stuck to the disc and the masking tape could be peeled off.

Vinyl cutouts

The disc was then placed in a bed of shaving cream mixed with Fabric color for synthetic materials and left for about 10 hours.

Disc in Bed of coloring agent

the disc was then washed with water and the vinyl design peeled off. The outcome of this process was a dark pinkish red disc with the intended design in white.

Finished design 1

The Beholster template was put on the latter disc using the same method but the disc was coloured using glue, instead of shaving foam and colour dripped in using a acetone solution mixed with the same colouring agent.

Part 2

First of we needed to perform a KERF test on the lazer cutter. A short report on the KERF test can be found on my team members website. My first idea was designing a plant shelf that could hang on a wall. this would have to hold together only by press fitted material. This would mean that the shelf will be have to be heavily reinforced in order to hold reliably together. This convinced me to instead design a small standing shelve. Perhaps one where larger pots could be placed above a smaller segment containing seedlings. That would allow excess water from the pots could leak down to water the seedlings.

A few things need to be considered before the designing process can begin: The material used needs to be able to withstand being soaked in water so using plastics instead of wood be preferable. The weigth of the upper pots means that the shelve will be top heavy, in order to counter that, the base of the shelve needs to be relatively thick. Lastly the amount of material used is limiting. I began by making a exact model of the flower pots I had at home and weighed them. They weighed together around 500 grams with plants, being recently watered. Below you can see the first draft of the shelf.

This seemed to work but before I could keep developing the design I made a simple structural simulation in Fusion 360. I did this by Setting the material of the shelf to clear acrylic, constrained the bottom of the feet of the shelf and put a force of 10N (2x the required ) on the top part. This gave this result

Simulation of force on shelf 1

The displacement of the shelf is negligible especially since the actual force distribution is not so uniform, but rather closer to the edges, minimizing the effect of the pots weight. Next version had the bottom shelf for the seedlings which still seemed to hold up with a safety factor of atleast 15 and minimal displacement.

The next step was getting rid of zones on the sides of the shelf which were not very affected by the load. A triangle was cut from the bottom feet and from the middle of the side, above the lower shelf.

Again the effects of the load were minimal. I settled on this design and began exporting the file to a format that the lasercutter could take. I used this video as a guide on how to do that.

The next step was using the lasercutter to cut the design out from a 500x500x3mm piece of clear acrylic. The program used was EpilogLaser. From Inkscape, the file was printed which took it to the Epilog program. There, the speed was set to 30%, at first, and the power was kept at 100%. The Lasercutter didn't go all the way through the material and the speed had to be lowered down to 20% and the program was run again over the first run. After assembling it turned out that the braces on the outside didn't fit over the nibs on the lower shelf. This caused the nibs to break because the acrylic is too brittle to bend in for the brases.

Here, the braces were 0.1mm wider in the vertical direction as their function is to make sure that the shelf isn't pulled through in the horizontal direction. This made it easier to fit around the nibs. This was not enough however and the nibs were redesigned.

These were made so that they didn't have to bend as much and as the brace slides on the transition is smoother. This new design was cut from a acrylic piece of the same thickness and the outcome was a lot better. Most of the braces slid on the nibs pretty easily and worked as designed, that is to fasten the shelf in the horizontal direction. Because the Lasercutter wasn't working properly, two of the braces had a small slit of material in the way of the nibs, causing them to break.

The rest of the braces still held the shelf so it could hold the whole contraption together. This is the final outcome where it can be seen that the pots fit perfectly.
